Day 7 - Warm-up

Perfectionists often stifle their confidence because they place unrealistic demands on themselves. Perfectionists, either consciously or subconsciously, set very strict expectations for performance. When they do not achieve their desired results, they become frustrated and their confidence level sinks like a lead balloon.

Extremely high expectations can make you feel like a failure regardless of how well you do. So, you ask, “How do I know when I have perfectionistic traits that may be holding me back?” Below are some of the more common signs that your perfectionism is adversely impacting your performance:

  • Intense work ethic that breeds extremely high expectations.
  • Easily frustrated when not performing well.
  • Belief that you must be perfect to perform well.
  • Tension or anxiety during your performance.
  • Can’t play in the present, (the here and now) and/or your focus is on the future and outcomes.
  • Self-critical behavior which results in feeling like you are never satisfied with your performance.

Day 7 - Daily Confidence Boost

Here is the conflict: Perfectionists think they have a good attitude but in reality, their attitude is poor due to the high demands and self-limiting beliefs they place on performance. Although some of the traits of perfectionism help athletes to succeed such as high goals, (which are different than unrealistic ones) determination, commitment, a strong work ethic, and not accepting mediocrity, perfectionists do not realize the disadvantages of perfectionism and how it can hurt confidence and cause anxiety.

The following are behaviors that perfectionists engage in that are detrimental to the mission of developing ultimate self-confidence:

  • Setting unrealistic or very strict expectations.
  • Dwelling on shortcomings.
  • Fretting over past mistakes.
  • Self-criticizing behavior.
  • Constantly making comparisons to others.
  • Over-analyzing performance.
  • Viewing performance as good or bad with no middle ground.
  • Measuring self-esteem based on sport-related success.
  • Focus on future performance gains instead of the playing in the present, (the “now”.)

All of the above self-limiting beliefs directly or indirectly jeopardize the development of ultimate self-confidence and affect your self-esteem. Because this is so important, you must uncover and question the value of such beliefs. The next step in today’s confidence plan is to help you abolish some negative perfectionistic tendencies and replace them with a more functional mindset. The famous psychologist, Albert Ellis, calls this, “developing a more rational philosophy.”

Day 7 - Energize Your Confidence

You will be guided through a short period of relaxation (an abdominal breathing exercise) followed by a simple guided mental imagery exercise. In addition, you will repeat the following affirmations to yourself three times during the guided imagery exercise.

  1. “I commit to striving for perfection in practice, but accept that I can’t be perfect in competition.”
  2. “I will perform functionally (win ugly and just get the job done) instead of placing super high demands on my performance.”
  3. “I choose to be my own best friend and let go of mistakes quickly without criticizing myself.”

Day 7 - Taking Confident Action

The goal is to identify any of the above disadvantages of perfectionism and work to change these traits. Dealing with perfectionism is rather complex and requires training in and of itself. However, it is still important to recognize and acknowledge the signs of perfectionistic behavior that have a negative impact on your performance.
