Day 14 - Warm-up

Patience is linked closely to confidence. A patient athlete is a confident athlete. Without patience, you are left with frustration and intolerance. Patience is the ingredient most needed when you hit the wall of adversity. Patience is a mental skill that is most needed when you are stuck in neutral and you can’t find any individual or team momentum./p>

Impatience happens when an athlete feels desperate and is not performing up to their expectations. Webster’s defines patience as, “steadfast, despite opposition, difficulty or adversity.” In sports, I translate steadfast to confidence; so being patient in sports is having confidence in your ability despite opposition, difficulty or adversity.

Patience is a form of confidence about the future that things will get better or improve with time if you wait for it. Athletes with a fragile state of confidence have trouble “waiting” for their performance to improve and thus lose patience and become frustrated easily.

Day 14 - Daily Objective

To learn how to have patience and confidence in the face of failure or adversity.

Day 14 - Daily Confidence Boost

The first step in improving patience is to understand when and how you become impatient during competition. Awareness is once again the key here. Awareness allows you to prepare yourself to cope effectively with challenging situations that test your patience. Use the three step model below to help improve your patience.

  1. Using the anticipation and cope model from Day 11, list the top five annoying situations in your sport that cause you to lose patience. For example, you keep making the same error and giving up the ball to the opposition. (Hint: these annoying situations could be mistakes you make, lack of results, or a competitor’s behavior or actions.)
  2. List the corresponding thought for each of the above situations. Using the example above, “I can’t stand giving up the ball. I am so frustrated because I hate getting the ball stolen.”
  3. Create your own personal coping method that will assist you in coping with adversity. This is similar to what you did in Day 11. Using the same example, “Stay calm, I can recover. Focus on making quality passes and everything will turn out.”

Below is another example of a coping plan to help you complete this exercise:

  • Situation:Double fault two times in a row.
  • Belief:“I can’t stand to double fault the game away. I am losing my cool over my crummy service game.”
  • Coping:“Let it go, it’s done with. Stay patient and focused on hitting quality serves. I know you can recover this game.”

Day 14 - Energize Your Confidence

You will be guided through a short period of relaxation (an abdominal breathing exercise) followed by a simple guided mental imagery exercise. In addition, you will repeat the following affirmations to yourself three times during the guided imagery exercise.

  1. “I will be flexible and adapt with patience to any challenge that is thrown at me.”
  2. “Even if the game does not go as expected, I choose to remain patient and wait for good things to happen.”
  3. “Success does not happen overnight; I must remain patient and stay confident for my chance to excel.”

Day 14 - Taking Confident Action

  • In your next competition, give yourself the luxury of making 3 small errors so you can play on with confidence.
  • Practice your coping strategies when you start to feel irritated and frustrated.
