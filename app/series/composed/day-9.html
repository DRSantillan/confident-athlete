Day 9 - Warm-up

I believe many athletes and performers limit their talents with so-called comfort zones, which is a type of mental barrier that is hard to break through. A comfort zone becomes a problem whenever an athlete is playing better than expected or is breaking new ground.

A comfort zone is a mental barrier an athlete sets in his own mind based on preconceived notions and expectations about what is and what is not possible to achieve.

Athletes from other sports also lose composure when performing beyond a comfort zone. Similar to the golfer in the example above, when an athlete or team feels out of place (winning a game they expected to lose) or in new uncharted territory, the tendency is to either become anxious and scared or defensive and protective. Composure is challenged when an athlete or team compares how they are performing to how they expect to perform.

Preconceived notions about what might be possible, likely, or typical for one’s performance turn into a mental barrier that is often hard to shake.

Therefore, part of developing maximum composure for crunch-time performances is the ability to perform without the mental handcuffs of a comfort zone.

Day 9 - Daily Composure Boost

Begin by assessing your behavior when affected by a comfort zone. We do this by recognizing the signs that you are limiting your success with a comfort zone. The mental barriers of a comfort zone can affect athletes in various ways. Below are the most common symptoms that a comfort zone is impacting your performance.

  • You get nervous, anxious, or afraid when playing better than expected.
  • You compare how you are presently doing with how you expected to do before you started the competition.
  • You “sit on your lead” and play defensively; not wanting to risk your current position, lead, score, etc.
  • You are in “protect-the-lead” mode, such as when the golfer doesn’t want to give back a shot, the race driver protects his lead, the tennis player doesn’t want to drop a game when up 5-0, and the hockey team tries to maintain its 3-0 lead.
  • You are in a hurry for the competition to finish because you don’t want your lead to disappear.
  • You doubt whether you can maintain your lead and lose self-confidence.
  • You worry about what others will think if/when you screw up a big lead.

Next, develop a solution by learning how to play without the mental constraints of a comfort zone. In Table 11 below, you will find a list of mental traps of the comfort zone in the left column. On the right, is a solution to each comfort zone mental trap. Your mission, if you choose to accept, is to match the correct mental strategy (fix) with the trap on the left.

Table 11. Matching Game: Fixing Your Mental Traps

Comfort Zone Trap
Mental Strategy
Trap 1. You get nervous, anxious, or afraid of playing with a lead or better than expected.
Fix A. Push yourself to stay aggressive when playing with a lead. Go with the same game that positioned you in the lead.
Trap 2. You constantly compare how you are doing with how you expected to do.
Fix B. Be confident that your game will thrive with the lead. Be a Tiger – try to increase your lead instead of doubting yourself.
Trap 3. You “sit on your lead” and play defensively; not wanting to risk your position, lead, score, etc.
Fix C. Convince yourself that what others think does not matter. Focus on your performance, not other people.
Trap 4. You are in protect-the-lead mode and are afraid to mess up.
Fix D. Play one play or shot at a time – don’t think about playing with a lead.
Trap 5. You rush to finish because you don’t want your lead to vanish.
Fix E. Slow down, breathe, and relish the moment. Focus on how you will finish strong and narrow your mind on success.
Trap 6. You doubt if you can maintain your lead and lose selfconfidence.
Fix F. Stop comparing how you are doing with your expected results. Engage with the process instead of judging your performance.
Trap 7. You worry about what others will think if you screw up a big lead.
Fix G. Keep pushing ahead and don’t stop to think about messing up your lead. Play like it’s the first point of the game or first shot of the round.

Day 9 - Collect Your Composure

Repeat the following affirmations three times during the guided imagery exercise.

  1. “I am responsible for restricting myself with comfort zones.”
  2. “I am in control of my emotions and can play through any barrier.”
  3. “I choose to focus on my performance rather than judge how I am doing.”

Day 9 - Action Plans For Composure

  • Note in your workbook any instances in which you were mentally challenged by a comfort zone. What were you thinking about and feeling at the time?
  • Apply the mental strategies in Table 11 above to help you break through your mental barriers or comfort zones.
