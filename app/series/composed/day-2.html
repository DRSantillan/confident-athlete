Day 2 - Warm-up

Competition is a bigger stage for many athletes and that means bigger stakes. The bigger the stakes, the harder it is to keep emotions in check. Actors refer to this state of mind, that is, being able to perform during practice but stumbling during performance, as “stage fright.” While the stage in acting may not be the same as it is for athletics, stage fright or performance anxiety can cause turbulence in the way that you play.

The pressure you feel to perform well does not come from your environment or the quality of your opponent. Pressure and stress result from how you perceive the quality of your opponent or importance of the game. Some athletes get excited about playing a rival, but others are scared to lose for many different reasons— rejection by team members, an angry response from a coach, or disappointment from parents.

If an athlete’s mental game foundation is weak, emotional composure will suffer too. An athlete with very high expectations for his performance, for example, is likely to become easily frustrated and lose control emotionally.

Below is a list of the top eight mental errors that can challenge your composure. In the next few days of the program, I will address each of the mental errors.

Top Mental Game Breakdowns that Lead to Reduced Composure

  1. Perfectionism
  2. Worrying too much about what others think
  3. Irrational Belief
  4. Fear of Failure
  5. Doubt and Lack of Confidence
  6. Comfort Zones
  7. Intimidation
  8. Dwelling on Errors

Day 2 - Composure Boost

What mental game errors do not support maximum composure in competition and practice? Table 4 helps you identify if you are committing a mental game error that is not healthy for your composure. Using the table below, place a check mark next to each statement that you have experienced at one time in your sport.

Table 4. Mental Breakdowns that Cause Poor Composure

Identifying Mental Game Errors
Perctionism
1. I become frustrated when I do not perform to my expectations.
2. I often try to perform perfectly during the game or match.
3. If the game does not go according to plan, I become upset.
Worry About What Others Think
1. I worry about embarrassing myself in front of others during sports.
2. I often think that other people might be evaluating my performance.
3. I worry about what others may say to me when I perform poorly.
Irrational Beliefs
1. I have thoughts that I will choke because I have done this in the past.
2. When bad luck causes mistakes, I think the world is against me.
3. If I have a poor start to the game, I believe I will struggle all day.
Fear of Failure
1. I become tight or anxious before competition from worrying too much about failure.
2. I want to win so badly that I worry how awful it would be not to win.
3. In a tight match or game, I perform scared, controlled, or overly cautious.
Doubt or Lack of Confidence
1. I question my ability or doubt myself prior to competition.
2. After a mistake, I doubt my ability to play well the rest of the game.
3. I have less confidence in myself than my teammates or competitors.
Comfort Zones
1. When playing with the lead, I become more cautious or tentative.
2. Mental scoring barriers or thoughts about a certain score I should achieve hold me back from reaching my potential.
3. I play safe and don’t take risks when I have made some errors.
Dwell of Errors
1. I have trouble letting go of past mistakes or I can’t get over the memory of mistakes I have made in the past.
2. Thinking about a past mistake makes me frustrated.
3. It takes me a few minutes to get over my errors and not think about them.
Intimidation
1. I compare myself to other athletes who I think are better than me.
2. When I think about my competition, I feel intimidated or inferior.
3. I do not like confrontation and avoid it when possible.

Day 2 - Collect Your Composure

  1. “I know what mental game breakdowns lead to a lack of self-control.”
  2. “I have the ability and commitment to improve my mental game foundation.”
  3. “I have a free will and am in complete control of my thoughts and emotions.”

The goal is to recognize when you are committing the mental game errors discussed previously and become more aware of your mental processes that undermine your composure. The remainder of the program will help you to develop a stronger mental game foundation that will lead to increased composure.
