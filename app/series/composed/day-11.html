Day 11 - Warm-up

In the last session we discussed how athletes become self-intimidated and introduced some strategies to help you perform free of intimidation. Athletes who intimidate themselves focus too much on other athletes and the situation rather than what is under his or her direct control. Thus, this lesson applies to helping you with intimidation as well as your general mindset for composure.

Do you spend too much energy worrying about things that you cannot control on the athletic field, court, or course? For example, have you ever been side-tracked because you were arguing with an official about a bad call he made even when you knew the official was not going to change his call? Most likely, you became upset or frustrated, lost your composure and it affected you for several minutes to follow or even the rest of the game.

Focusing on things that are beyond your control is a distraction at the very least. As we discussed in the case of intimidation, focusing too much on your opponent’s record or abilities can lead to composure breakdowns. We can classify all resultoriented thinking about outcomes into thoughts or “events” that are beyond your direct control (in the present moment). Athletes who focus too much on results, instead of the process, are likely to become anxious, scared, worried, and perform tentatively at best.

As part of your plan for developing maximum composure, you must train yourself to focus only on the stuff in your personal and immediate control. Thus, we can break down the competitive arena into two areas:

  • Things that you control: your thoughts, behaviors, performance, and reactions in both practice and competition.
  • Things beyond your control: events or persons that you cannot influence during competition and which cause you to become sidetracked from your real mission such as a heckler in the stands or rude competitors.

Day 11 - Composure Clip

“I don’t get upset over things I can’t control, because if I can’t control them there’s no use getting upset. And I don’t get upset over the things I can control, because if I can control them there’s no use in getting upset.”~Mickey Rivers, MBL Player

Day 11 - Daily Objective

To identify the things in your sport that you can control and learn to not pay attention to events or people in which you can’t control.

The next step is to get more specific with the factors you can control in your specific sport and to identify the events or thoughts that cause you to get side tracked during practice or competition. I’ll use the sport of golf to highlight some specific examples of factors that you can and cannot control:

Factors you can control in putting

  • Your preshot routine
  • Your plan for the putt or read
  • The images you use during the routine
  • Your general mental preparation for the putt

Factors you cannot control in putting

  • The condition of the greens
  • How your playing partners behave
  • The weather conditions
  • The effects of wind, spike marks on the green, and the “lumpy donut” or shoe prints around the hole
  • The final outcome of the putt

Day 11 - Collect Your Composure

Repeat the following affirmations three times during the guided imagery exercise.

  1. “I will choose to only concern myself with things I can control or influence.”
  2. “I will focus on what I have to do to execute well in the process.”
  3. “I will not allow things that I can’t change to zap or drain my energy.”

Day 11 - Action Plans For Composure

  • Begin to structure your preparation for your sport on the factors you can directly control in your sport. Be prepared to refocus your attention when you recognize that you are obsessing about something that is beyond your control.
  • After each competition, review your performance and assess your ability to stay focused on what you can control or impact.
