Day 4 - Warm-up

Social approval is a need to be accepted, respected, or liked by others, which is a very common behavior in today’s society. Research done by Wake Forest University shows that social approval and disapproval influences how people feel about themselves./p>

Social approval is an important phenomenon in my discussions with athletes that I coach. Many athletes rely too much on social approval to boost their own levels of self-worth. Some athletes think that if others respect their sports performance, this, for some reason, will make them a better person. Many athletes buy into this notion and think that they are better people if they can achieve acknowledgement, gain approval or respect from others through sports.

How is social approval related to emotional composure? The need for approval from others becomes another source of pressure for many athletes. Athletes that crave approval from others to feel better or more confident, often become anxious in competition because the opportunity for embarrassment is present.

What happens when an athlete badly wants respect or admiration from others? He or she is afraid to lose approval points by playing poorly or making mistakes. This sounds like fear of failure and it is a certain type of fear of failure. However, most of the time, craving approval causes you to read the minds of others and not really know what they are actually thinking - just unsubstantiated mind reading!

Day 4 - Daily Objective

To understand how social approval relates to composure and teach you how to stop worrying so much about what others think about your performance.

Day 4 - Daily Composure Boost

The first step is to seek out any approval cravings you might have. People are motivated for many reasons and gaining approval from others is one of them. Below are the top seven categories of social approval that impact athletes:

  1. Want others to see you as an achieving or successful athlete.
  2. Want others to respect you for your abilities as an athlete./li>
  3. Want to have a certain status from a group of athletes.
  4. Want social recognition from fans, media, or coaches.
  5. Want to impress or make others happy, such as a parent.
  6. Want to avoid feeling embarrassed and having others think badly of you.
  7. Want to avoid performing poorly for fear of not gaining respect.

Using the list above, write down the top two or three needs for approval you have experienced in the past, knowingly or unknowingly. It could be a variation of any one of the seven statements above:

Once you identify the source of approval that you desire, consciously or unconsciously, you can tackle the issue head on. As you can see, some are desires (i.e., want respect from others) and some can be classified as fears (i.e., fear of embarrassment). Both have equal power to control you in competition.

Given the two or three needs you identified above, the goal is to “convince” yourself that these needs are not relevant to your performance and should be a non-issue. To overcome the mental obstacle social approval creates is to debate the rationality of these needs and replace with a more effective belief system. Your objectives of this exercise are to:

  1. Debate the rationality of a certain need you identified.
  2. Stop “mind reading” into what you think others think.
  3. Learn to approve of yourself and have self-respect and self-confidence.
  4. Learn to focus in the now, instead of worrying about what others may think if you perform well or poorly.

Day 4 - Collect Your Composure

  1. “I choose to stop trying to read minds about what they may think.”
  2. “I give myself unconditional approval no matter if I win, lose, or draw.”
  3. “I will quickly refocus on my performance when I worry about others’ views.”

Day 4 - Action Plans For Composure

  • The next time you have practice or competition, begin to notice when you engage in mind reading and note it later in your workbook.
  • See how often you start to crave approval from others during a week of practice.
  • Implement your “Stop the Mind Reading” strategies outlined in Table 6 every day when you notice yourself worrying too much about what others think.
