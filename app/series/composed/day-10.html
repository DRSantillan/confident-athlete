Day 10 - Warm-up

What do you think about when I mention intimidation? Do you think about that bully at school who intimidated all his classmates? Do you visualize becoming intimidated by an opponent that is verbally harassing you? Or, do you think about your own inner bully that uses intimidation to sabotage your performance?

Some athletes resort to intentional intimidation such as verbal abuse or even bullying. Some athletes become intimidated by their own negative thoughts or fears that turn into pressure and mental gremlins. These “internal” intimidators are detrimental to peak performance and are known as self-intimidation.

Self-intimidation is what you bring on yourself because you worry about your competition or the team you are about to play. From my perspective, selfintimidation is the most common type of intimidation in sports and is also the hardest intimidation to overcome. Why? You can easily ignore what others say to you, but you can’t ignore your own self-talk!

Confident and composed athletes usually do not intimidate themselves. Instead, they are in control of their emotions and love the challenge of testing their skills against others athletes. The bigger and better the challenge, the more enjoyment they gain from the competition.

Athletes who become self-intimidated feel anxious, scared, and pressured to perform their best if they want to win. They worry about performing against other athletes who are just as skilled. They sometimes compare themselves to better athletes. Making comparisons to athletes who you think are better than you, causes a lack of confidence and produces feelings of inferiority.

Day 10 - Daily Composure Boost

Athletes can intimidate themselves in many ways, but it usually deals with how you perceive your skills compared to the skills of the competition. The most common ways that you may feel intimidated are:

  • Your opponent has an intimidating presence or look of confidence
  • You do not feel like you belong, you feel out of place or lack confidence
  • The reputation or record of your opponent precedes him or her
  • You are in awe or put others on a pedestal or are “star-struck”
  • You have advanced to a higher level of competition where everyone is just as good as you are if not better
  • You are in awe of the situation or environment

As you can imagine, self-intimidation happens when you give your opponent too much power or attention by putting your competitors on a pedestal. When you feel intimidated you may relate to the following:

  1. Anxious or scared about outcomes
  2. Doubt or lack of confidence in your ability
  3. Feel as if you don’t belong at the event
  4. Want to go hide and slip away unnoticed
  5. Afraid to embarrass yourself

No More Intimidation!

It should be clear to you now that self-intimidation happens because you either (1) focus too much on the competition or (2) you compare your skills, ability, or history to that of your competitors. Your first task is to avoid making comparisons to your competitors, which means you should not concern yourself with the reputation or status of whom you will compete against. Your second task is to avoid focusing on your competition at all unless your observations are important for strategy or game plans.

With self-intimidation, most of this is in your mind. That means you are choosing to focus on the wrong thoughts and images prior to and during competition. Most intimidation begins prior to competition as you gawk in awe or worry about your inability to beat your opponent.

If it begins in the mind, we can stop it before intimidating thoughts get out of control. I don’t like to go on a “no” campaign, but I think this lesson warrants drastic measures! Complete your own “stop the internal bully” statements at the end of Table 12.

Day 10 - Collect Your Composure

Repeat the following affirmations three times during the guided imagery exercise.

  1. “No one can intimidate me, but myself.”
  2. “I choose to fight my internal bully and become the intimidator.”
  3. “I love the challenge of testing my skills against the best!”

Day 10 - Action Plans For Composure

  • You must become more aware of the feelings and thoughts you have about your opponents, which cause self-intimidation. Note these situations in your workbook.
  • Apply your “fight the internal bully” statements when you begin to give your opponent too much energy or attention.
