Day 7 - Warm-up

Several mental game challenges are linked to fear of failure, such as low selfconfidence, worry about making mistakes, fear of rejection or embarrassment, and several others. One of the problems associated with the fear of failure is an intense need to avoid failure, which causes you to think too much about not making mistakes or avoiding pain; or causes you to perform cautiously, scared, or too defensively.

One of the goals of The Composed Athlete is to help you perform fearlessly, without the mental traps that hold back performance potential. One way to accomplish this is to help you adopt a success-driven mindset that focuses on what you want to accomplish and helps you strive for positive outcomes.

In the last session you identified your specific fear of failure(s). Today, you’ll learn how to replace avoidance behavior with a success-driven mindset.

Day 7 - Daily Composure Boost

A success driven- approach focuses on what you want to achieve and task-relevant performance cues. It’s a focus in the moment, the here and now, and what you have to do to achieve excellence, while concurrently having a positive goal in mind. Athletes who take a fear-driven approach to their sport become paralyzed by trying to avoid failure, pain, or mistakes.

In table 10, I have listed common statements or beliefs athletes maintain that are avoidance behaviors. Your task is to become an expert at changing fear-driven statements into a success-driven statements.

Day 7 - Collect Your Composure

Repeat the following affirmations three times during the guided imagery exercise.

  1. “I no longer concern myself with avoiding mistakes in competition.”
  2. “I am a success-driven athlete and focus on what I want to happen.”
  3. “I can recognize when I slip into avoidance mode and can adjust.”

Day 7 - Action Plans For Composure

  • Prior to each practice or competition, I want you to set three success-driven goals to help you achieve what you want to accomplish and not what you are trying to avoid.
  • After each competition, evaluate how often you embraced a success-driven approach. Also, note the situations in which you slipped back into a failure driven or avoidance approach.
