Day 8 - Warm-up

You can’t perform your best or have maximum composure unless you have a strong foundation of self-confidence. I often discuss how doubt is the polar opposite of confidence – if you doubt your ability, you don’t have full confidence. Likewise, when you are not confident in your ability to perform well it opens the door for you to feel anxious, scared, worried, or apprehensive about your performance or the outcome of competition.

What is self-confidence and how do you become confident? Self-confidence is simply the strength of your belief that you can successfully execute a skill in the present moment (for more information on self-confidence see The Confident Athlete Program). Self-confidence is knowing you have done the homework preparation through training and practice to succeed at your level and can win.

Many forms of confidence exist. Confidence in your ability to stay composed is another important step to be in control of your emotions and react appropriately to mistakes and errors. The opposite can also be true in that some athletes repeatedly choke, become unglued emotionally in crunch time, and can never seem to be able to finish off a close competition. Thus they have very low self-confidence in their ability to display and apply the fundamentals of composure.

Day 8 - Daily Objective

To learn how to develop a positive self-image of yourself as a composed athlete and improve your confidence in your mental game ability to perform with composure.

Day 8 - Daily Composure Boost

Do you see yourself as a choker? Do you view yourself as someone that crumbles under the pressure? Do you think that you are a hothead and have no control over your emotions after setbacks or mistakes? If you answered yes to any of these questions, then you need to change how you view yourself (self-image) as a performer or athlete.

Because you purchased The Composed Athlete program I suspect that you want to make positive changes to gain, keep and maintain your composure. This requires that I help you to (1) improve your self-image as a composed athlete and (2) help you gain confidence in yourself as a composed athlete, which is the overall aim of this program.

Lets start by identifying some negative self-labels (names you give yourself that are not helpful), which undermine any confidence you may have in yourself as a composed athlete. Read the statements below. Can you identify with any of these negative self-labels or statements?

Negative Self-Labels about Composure

  • I choked in the past and I will continue to be a choker.
  • I am not a big game player; I never perform well in big games.
  • Others call me a hot head; I must be a hot head.
  • I’m a Type-A athlete; I can’t relax and play my game.
  • I crumble under the pressure of a big lead and find a way to lose.
  • When I perform, I feel as though I am going to see the executioner because I am so scared to play badly.
  • I am “Mr. Tension” on the playing field and never seem to relax and perform free during competition.

Although extreme examples, these statements show the negative affect that a poor self-image has on your performance. Your athletic self-image dictates your behavior in competition and you will default to this type of thinking when you are under pressure or adversity.

Day 8 - Collect Your Composure

Repeat the following affirmations three times during the guided imagery exercise.

  1. “I am gaining confidence in my ability to thrive in crunch time.”
  2. “I can clearly imagine myself as the crunch time performer.”
  3. “My image of myself as a mentally tough athlete under pressure improves every day.”
