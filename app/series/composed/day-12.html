Day 12 - Warm-up

Emotional control is essential to peak performance in sports. Emotional control is a huge facet of composure. So far, the lessons have focused on dealing with competitive pressure, intimidation, and anxiety. Every athlete at one time has become upset, frustrated, or angry at his or herself (or other people). How you perform will depend on your ability to let go and stay composed after errors.

Emotional control and letting go of errors is such an important mental skill in sports psychology that I have devoted two lessons to this topic. Many fine athletes sabotage their own performance because they do not have the skills to cope with adversity and have not learned the skills to control their emotions. Highly motivated athletes with perfectionistic tendencies are particularly prone to becoming upset and frustrated with mistakes or when not performing up to expectations.

What is emotional control? Emotional control is the ability to stay even tempered, level headed, or have a calm mind when you are challenged by mishaps or adversity. Even the top athletes in the world can become upset with their performance, but they are able to stay in control and get back to business or execution quickly, and that’s what separates the great athletes who recover from mistakes from the good athlete who cracks and is lost for the remainder of the competition.

One important point to keep in mind: No one person (or event that occurs) can make you feel frustrated but yourself! Yes, you might feel frustrated by striking out when your team most needs you, or missing a short putt that costs you the match win. However, you must understand that it’s not the strike out or missed putt that causes frustration, rather it’s your belief or perception about these events that creates frustration.

Day 12 - Daily Composure Boost

I teach my students to approach emotional composure two ways: (1) taking a supportive pregame mindset into competition and (2) learning to let go of errors before emotions snowball out of control. You have to both prepare an accepting mindset before you compete and arm yourself with mental strategies to cope your best.

What is the correct mindset you should take to competition to be more accepting of yourself? A mindset void of expectation and free of judgment for starters. The first step is to identify strict expectations that cause you to lose your composure when you think that you expectations are not met. One of your mental game objectives is to play without expectations that can cause you to become easily frustrated if you don’t match your own expectations.

Expectations that can lead to feelings of frustration include:

  • I must play perfectly to be successful today.
  • I expect to perform perfectly today and if I don’t, I am worthless.
  • I cannot make any mistakes if I want to win.
  • To play my best, I must have an error-free performance.
  • I expect my teammates to be perfect and not make errors.
  • I should get upset when I make errors to show others that I am really a better athlete.
  • I won’t encounter any bad luck.
  • If I make a mistake, I should automatically be upset or frustrated.

Once you are aware of the expectations that cause you to feel frustrated and dwell, you can agree to throw these away and replace with manageable goals you can achieve during your performance. It goes like this:

State the Expectation:
Replace with a Manageable Goal:
“I can’t make any mistakes if I want to win today.”
“My mini goal is to have 4 quality shots on goal today.”

Part of mental preparation is preparing your mind to cope with anything that could happen during competition in which would cause you to lose your composure or become frustrated. You can start by reviewing your past performances when you lost your composure. Has an opponent tried to psych you out with negative comments? Have you ever lost control on a referee for making a bad call? Do you get upset when your coach yells at you for mistakes? You get the idea.

Day 12 - Collect Your Composure

Repeat the following affirmations three times during the guided imagery exercise.

  1. “No one can make me feel frustrated, but myself.”
  2. “I choose to compete with a non-judgmental mindset free of expectations.”
  3. “Errors are just errors and nothing more, I can react with composure.”

Day 12 - Action Plans For Composure

  • Your goal is to note any expectations or strict beliefs that may set you up for feeling frustrated if you do not perform up to them. Agree to leave these in the locker room and replace them with manageable goals that focus your mind on execution.
  • After each competition, note any instances where you were upset because you made an error or got a bad call from an official. How did you react to each trying situation?
