Day 6 - Warm-up

Fear of failure is a huge topic in sports psychology because many over-motivated, perfectionist athletes have some form of fear of failure. What is fear of failure?

The best athletes in the world perform without the constraints of fear. But, that is because they have learned to master and control their thoughts, so that they can remain focused in the present, instead of dwelling on the past, or anticipating the future.

Several mental game issues are linked to fear of failure, such as low selfconfidence, needless worry about making mistakes, fear of rejection or embarrassment, and the list goes on.

Fears related to the fear of failure can be both rational (I don’t want to fall off the high bar”) and irrational (“I’m worried that others won’t like me if I’m not perfect”). Thus, today’s lesson ties in with your previous lesson on irrational beliefs.

To identify the specific fears you might harbor related to the fear of failure and learn how to let go of your fears that hold you back from reaching peak performance.

Day 6 - Daily Composure Boost

Step one is to identify the typical fears that athletes maintain in sports today. Most of these fears are born out of an intense need or desire to succeed. It may seem odd that fear of failure actually comes from a strong desire to succeed, but most fear of failure starts with a very motivated and determined individual who badly wants to win, achieve status, or be recognized for their achievements. The problem, however, is that this desire to win or be recognized actually results in the athlete focusing too much on the future and freezing up out of fear that his or her goals won’t be met.

What are the common fears associated with the fear of failure?

  • Fear of losing a match, game, or race
  • Fear of embarrassment
  • Fear of letting others down
  • Fear of not playing up to one’s potential
  • Fear of not meeting expectations of others
  • Fear of exhaustive efforts and not receiving any “pay off” for the hard work
  • Fear of negative social evaluation
  • Fear of not being accepted by others or not gaining approval

The more you want to succeed, the harder it is to handle failure and the more you avoid it. In table 9, please check off the statements that are applicable to you.

Table 9. Top 10 Symptoms of Fear of Failure

Fear of Failure Signs
1. You have very strict expectations for your performance.
2. You perform better in practice than in competition.
3. You have pre-game anxiety or worry about your performance.
4. You don’t have the same self-confidence in competition as in practice.
5. You want so badly to succeed that this causes anxiety.
6. You focus on results too much and can’t focus in the moment.
7. You are impatient with results in competition and improvement.
8. You attach your self-esteem to your level of performance.
9. You often care too much about what others think.
10. You are afraid to let others down (coaches, parents, teammates).

The underlying causes for fear of failure can be classified into two main categories:

  1. Fear of negative results or consequences (e.g. Fear of losing, fear of not performing during crunch time, etc.)
  2. Fear of not getting approval, acceptance or respect from others (Fear of letting down the team, coach, spectators or a parent.)

If you have identified three or more signs of fear of failure on the test in table 9, it is safe to say you have some form of fear of failure. Questions to consider:

  • Are you afraid of what others may think of how you are performing?
  • Are you afraid of investing your time into practice and hard work, but in the end not reaping the benefit of your extended practices?
  • Do you want to win so badly that you become anxious about the outcome and your performance suffers?

Once you identify the specific fear, you must address it head on instead of just recognizing and trying to mask the symptoms in which fear manifests itself such as, anxiety, tension, and over-cautious play. Use reason and rationality to convince yourself that the fear is irrational and not based on fact.

Tips for Challenging your Fear of Failure:

  • Dispute the importance of your fear in the bigger picture of life. Does it really matter when you put it into perspective?
  • Question the rationality of your fear – is it grounded in reality?
  • Does holding on to this fear help you perform better or is it a functional necessity to your success?
  • Are you procrastinating – hoping your fear will disappear on its own?
  • Is this fear an absolute (written in stone) with no options to change?
  • Do others encourage you to hold on to this belief unknowingly?

Essentially, fear of failure focuses on all the wrong areas to be successful in sports. In the next lesson, I discuss how to be success-driven, not fear of failure driven.
