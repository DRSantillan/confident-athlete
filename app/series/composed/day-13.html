Day 13 - Warm-up

In the last lesson, I introduced the concept of emotional control for dealing with setbacks and why this is important for composure. Emotional composure is enhanced when you take a non-judgmental and accepting attitude into competition. Emotional control is the ability to stay even tempered, level headed, or have a calm mind even when you are challenged by mishaps or mistakes. But, what few athletes recognize is that emotional control strategies begin before you enter competition.

Many athletes perceive that they are victims of their circumstances – if the tennis player double faults, for example, it’s the double fault that causes emotional turmoil. The problem lies in being reactive instead of proactive when faced with adversity during a game. If you buy into the belief: “I have no control over my emotions and I cannot help feeling upset when the game does not happen according to my plan,” you are prone to lose control. This irrational belief causes you to feel helpless without any control over your circumstances.

I teach my students that they have a free-will and have the choice to react to adversity with composure instead of helplessness or out of negative habits. This means using proactive strategies to cope with adversity instead of reacting out of ineffective habits. Proactive athletes plan and strategize how they will cope with mishaps or trying situations when they aren’t in the midst of the emotions of the crisis moment. They learn preset mental strategies to help them stay composed and react with emotional stability – and this is the goal of today’s lesson.

Borrowing from a famous psychologist Albert Ellis, he would say that, people are not frustrated or upset about by events that happen to them, but rather they become upset or disturbed by the way people think about them. He would say that the fact you make a “mistake” such as a three-putt is not frustrating in itself. Rather, frustration comes from how you perceive the “event” and what beliefs you maintain about the event or mistake.

This means we need to modify the old habits or ways of thinking about mistakes so you can be more composed. This requires that you learn how to disagree with (or dispute) your thoughts or beliefs about mistakes and replace these with more functional ways of thinking, which will lead to improved emotional control.

4 Steps to Better Emotional Control:

  1. List an event or mistake that causes you frustration.
  2. Identify the beliefs or thoughts you maintain that cause you frustration.
  3. Replace the belief or thoughts with ones that will help you play on with composure.
  4. Proactively practice your coping strategies (or new beliefs) prior to competition.

Here is an example of the process:

  1. The mistake: 3-putting in golf
  2. The belief that causes frustration: “I am too good to 3-putt from 30 feet. I will never shoot a good score if I keep doing that!”
  3. Your revised thinking about this mistake: “Even the best players in the world 3-putt occasionally. I will recover and putt well the rest of the day.”
  4. Practice this coping strategy in your regular training. You can also imagine making the mistake and applying your new thinking process.

The goal is to improve your thinking about mistakes so you can play on with composure. I tell my students to find a way to make the mistakes “OK” in their own mind. This is an accepting mindset. I am not saying mistakes are OK, I am pointing out the fact that you must accept it – any way you can – so you can move on with a free mind to the next shot, play, or point.

Day 13 - Collect Your Composure

Repeat the following affirmations three times during the guided imagery exercise.

  1. “Mistakes are just mistakes. Only I make myself feel frustrated.”
  2. “I choose to accept mistakes as a part of being human and move on to the next play, shot, or point.”
  3. “I will perform with a non-judgmental and accepting mindset.”

Day 13 - Action Plans For Composure

  • Begin to apply and practice your new coping responses to mistakes on a daily basis.
  • Note in your workbook any new situations that caused you to lose composure during competition. Develop a new coping response for this situation so you can be prepared the next time.
