Day 5 - Warm-up

I want to dig down deeper into your mind and uncover another type of mental breakdown that causes you to feel pressure and then sabotages your performance – irrational beliefs.

Irrational beliefs are thoughts, feelings, ideas, attitudes, or ingrained ways of behaving that are self-defeating and often not based on reality. They are often made-up stories in your mind, and similar to the process of mind reading, create additional stress, anxiety, or worry about your performance.

How are irrational beliefs related to composure? Let me give you a real-life example:

At the time I was drafting The Composed Athlete, I received a phone call from one of my racing students who was dealing with the issue of irrational beliefs. His challenge was that he viewed winning as success and anything less as failure. He was using all or none thinking. If he was passed on the track or did not have a chance to win, he became frustrated and stopped trying altogether. Needless to say, his irrational beliefs were chipping away at his ability to perform. As you can see, this type of thinking can sabotage any athlete.

Day 5 - Daily Objective

To understand and eradicate irrational beliefs that cause undue pressure and lead to a loss of emotional control or self-composure.

Day 5 - Daily Composure Boost

The first goal is to become aware of your own self-defeating beliefs – this is a big step in itself – you must be able to put your finger on the beliefs that cause you to feel anxious or frustrated. The next step is to change your beliefs and develop a new attitude or a new philosophy about your sport, so you can remove the mental handcuffs.

The Top Irrational Beliefs in Sports:

  1. Perfectionism
  2. Attaching Self-Worth to Achievement
  3. Self-Conscious Behavior
  4. All or None Thinking
  5. Over-Generalization
  6. Negative Self-Labels

Below in Table 7, I have listed the top six irrational or unhealthy beliefs - identified above. Your task is to identify your own unhealthy beliefs, based on the examples provided and the questions posed in this exercise.

Your next task is to change your unhealthy beliefs that cause anxiety or pressure into healthy statements. This can be a difficult task if the belief is one that you have held on to all your life.

The process begins by questioning yourself about these beliefs, a personal rebuttal to your own beliefs. Below is an example….

  • Unhealthy belief: “If I don’t finish first in the race, I am a loser.”
  • Rebuttal and healthy belief: “Winning does not make me a better person and losing does not mean I am a loser in life. I am not perfect and can’t win every race I enter. I accept this. I am a good person no matter if I win or lose in sport.”

Look carefully at the beliefs that stifle your ability to perform with composure, confidence, focus, and consistency. Once you can identify these negative beliefs, it is easier to accept that change is necessary. Then, it is simply a matter of adapting your mindset by replacing irrational beliefs with healthy ones by taking an objective or rational approach.

Day 5 - Collect Your Composure

Repeat the following affirmations three times during the guided imagery exercise.

  1. “I can accept the fact that I have some ineffective beliefs I must change.”
  2. “I know I can change my unhealthy beliefs into healthy beliefs.”
  3. “I will commit to replacing my irrational ideas with healthy beliefs.”

Day 5 - Action Plans For Composure

  • After competition, review how you performed with your new way of thinking about your sports. Were you successful? Was it hard to adopt the new statements?
