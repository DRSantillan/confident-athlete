Day 7 - Warm-up

You either strive for success will strive to avoid failure and pain, what combination of both.One of the signs of fear of failure is an athlete and worries too much about making mistakes. If you do this, you play to carefully or cautiously. In this mindset, you're trying not to lose and try not to make mistakes. The golfer tries not to hit the ball in the water. The skater avoids falling on her jobs. The tennis player avoids missing the overhead volley. And, so forth.

Fear causes you to focus on what you don’t want to happen. Your performance is based on a state of tension, indecision, and carefulness - none of which make for optimum performance!

When performing tentatively, you body does not flow. You cannot execute your skills the way you trained yourself to do. Worry about mistakes leads to a lack of trust, which I will discuss in detail in Days 10-13.

Day 7 – Objective

To learn how to focus on positive execution instead of worrying about making mistakes or losing. To learn how to set process goals to help you stay focused on execution instead of outcomes.

Day 7 - Fearless Strategy

Today’s lesson is very important especially if you focus too much on mistakes and avoiding mistakes when you perform. You just cannot perform fearlessly and tension-free in this mindset. Start by understanding any times where you become tentative or focus too much on what not to do. Today’s exercise is to determine when you perform in a defensive mindset. I have included a few examples to help you get started. Check off the examples that apply to you…

Table 10. Times when I place scared or tentatively

If I make mistakes early in a game, I avoid making more mistakes.
If someone I respect is watching me, I want to impress him or her.
If I am playing in a tight match or game and it's crunch-time.
If I worry about disappointing my parents, coach, or teammates.
If I worry about losing a lead when I am ahead.
If my coach is shouting at me telling me not make any more mistakes.
If I worry that my opponent is better than me.
If I feel under-prepared or lack confidence due to a poor warm-up.

Next, learn how to focus on what is really important to execution. The best way to do this is to set small process goals for each competition. These help you focus on what TO DO instead of what not to do. I use two types of process goals to help athletes focus on what to do:

  • Performance goals: goals based on something you can measure such as number of rebound, block shops, or fairways hit the regulation.
  • Mental goals: goals based on your mental game and help you focus on one play, shot, or routine at a time, such as committing to a plane or visualising your pitch before you start the wind up.

One note here… I only use process goals to help you become immersed in execution – in the present moment. Although it’s rewarding to reach your goals, I don’t want you to evaluate or judge whether you’re on track to reach them. If you do this, you are not focusing on the process – you are turning them into expectations. Goals become expectations when you judge and criticize yourself for not reaching your goals.

Day 7 - Success Mottos

Repeat the following affirmationstwo times during the guided imagery exercise

  1. “I choose to focus on what I want, not fear what I think will happen.”
  2. “Mistakes happen in competition. How I respond to mistakes matters most.”
  3. “I commit to using process goals to help me focus on positive execution.”

Day 7 - Action Plans

  1. Continue to record (and become aware) of the times that you focus on what not to do (an avoidance mindset). Record these in Table 11 above.
  2. Prior to each practice and competition, set one or two performance and mental game process goals.
