Day 11 - Warm-up

You probably spend too much time in a practice or training mindset as discussed in the previous lesson. Therefore, one of your priorities is to learn how to stay confident and in control of yourself when your performance is not perfect or when you do not have your A game. An important lesson I teach my students is to learn how to perform efficiently instead of perfectly. I call this a “functional mindset.”

A functional mindset is the opposite of trying to make everything perfect. It starts with the idea that you DO NOT have to be perfect to perform your best. You are human and humans can’t be perfect. You will make mistakes and you have to accept mistakes. Tennis coach to professional players, Brad Gilbert, calls the functional mindset “winning ugly,” which he wrote a book about.

In golf, for example, winning ugly is called scrambling. A golfer finds a way to get the ball in the hole without regard for “the proper way.” In today’s lesson, you will learn how to adopt the functional mindset so you can play efficiently and get the job done in competition.

To help you learn how to perform with a functional mindset instead of worrying that your performance in not perfect.

Day 11 - Fearless Strategy

The following are the top six strategies to helping you perform in a functional mindset and win ugly.

  • Strive for perfection in practice, but accept that you cannot be perfect with your performance. It’s irrational to think you can be perfect with your performance. You can strive for perfection in your practice, but you have to accept errors as a natural part of sports, and part of being human.
  • Give yourself permission to make errors when performing. I don’t want you to expect that you will make errors, but you certainly must accept them when you commit errors. Give yourself the luxury of making 3-4 errors for each performance so you can play on in a functional mindset.
  • Perform efficiently instead of perfectly. This means that you have to abandon the “correct” way to perform in some cases. Use whatever works to help you get the job done in competition. For example, instead of needing to execute a play exactly from the playbook, be happy with a play that worked well, but maybe was not “textbook” execution.
  • Simplify how you perform. This is the opposite of trying to be perfect. Simplicity means using fewer thoughts to perform, reacting more, and NOT thinking about every single instruction your coach has told you. Don’t “overcoach” yourself, in other words. Less is always better.
  • Perform a task that you know you can perform well. Stick to your “bread and butter” play, shot, or routine. For example, I teach my golf students to play the shot that’s currently working well instead of the fancy or perfect shot.
  • Save practice for after the competition. Avoid analyzing your mistakes and trying to fix or correct your technique in the middle of a game or performance.
