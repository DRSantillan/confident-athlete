Day 14 - Warm-up

when I discuss balance with my students I use a simple, but important motto: "Leave sports on the playing field after you leave the court". Can tou forget about your performance after you leave the playing field, court or court? do you get frustrated when perform poorly that you take the feeling of disappointment home with you? And the very next day you are back aat practice and think you need to give more effort and preactice harder? Finally, does how well you play on a given day determine how you feel about yourself after you go home? Are you upset for a long time or can you leave it on the court?

Athletes who care deeply about sports success and who spend a lit of time in practice or training, such as tennis, for example, often wrongly define themselves through their sport. They believe that their level of success(as an Athlete) defines them as a person. these athletes live and die with every performance. After a poor performance, they feel like a failure.

If you win or perform well, do you feel better about yourself as a person? And if you lose or perform poorly, do you get upset, frustrated, and cannnot stand to look into the mirror when you get home? Sadly I hear these statements a lot.

If you train all day long, every day or take a 24/7 approach to your tennis training, you might be the type of athlete who defines yourself through sports.

If your life is all about tennis, you are at greater risk for self-esteem problems because your define yourself based on your level of success in tennis and cant seperate the different roles in your life. Your goal is to strive to find a balance in your life with family, school, spoerts, friends, hobbies and other career aspirations.

  • Become a Magician at "Role Playing" When you are playing tennis, involve yourself in that role - ccommit to 8 hours of practice or playing a day if thats what it takes, but when you leave the tennis court, switch roles and fully commit to other areas of your life. Dont superimpose the role of athlete onto other areas of life.
  • Define Your Self-Concept Outside of SportsTo help you become a magician at changing roles, you must first define whou you are outside tennis(use appendix 1 to help you with this task.) Use only descriptions that apply to your personal characteristics that you bring to every aspect of your life. Make a list of these positive characteristics and review them every day. Know who the person inside is!
  • Dont take it home with you.Leave tennis on the court. I know this one will be hard to do. At the very least, designate one or two hours of family or recreation time. Everyone needs a break. Leaving it on the court is especially important when you did not perform up to your ability. In addition, your family will appreciate you if you dont bring home the negative emotions.
