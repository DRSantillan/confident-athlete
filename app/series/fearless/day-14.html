Day 14 - Warm-up

When I discuss balance with my students I use a simple, but important motto: “Leave sports on the playing field after you leave the arena.” Can you forget about your performance after you leave the playing field, court, track, or course? Do you get so frustrated when you perform poorly that you take the feeling of disappointment home with you? And the very next day you are back at practice and think you need to give more effort and practice harder? Finally, does how well you play on a given day determine how you feel about yourself after you go home? Are you upset for a long time or can you leave it on the playing field?

Athletes who care deeply about sports success and who spend a lot of time in practice or training, such as golfers, skaters, swimmers, and gymnasts, for example, often wrongly define themselves through their sport. They believe that their level of success (as an athlete) defines them as a person. These athletes live and die with every performance. After a poor performance, they feel like a failure.

If you win or perform well, do you feel better about yourself as a person? And if you lose or perform poorly, do you get upset, frustrated, and can’t stand to look into the mirror when you get home? Sadly I hear these statements a lot!

If you train all day long, every day or take a 24/7 approach to your sport training, you might be the type of athlete who defines yourself through sports.

Day 14 - Objective

The goal of today’s lesson is to help you find greater balance in your life and to learn how to leave your performance on the playing field after the game.

Day 14 - Fearless Strategy

The first step to creating balance in your life is to understand that you are person and not just an athlete. You are not an “athlete” who happens also to be a person. You also happen to play sports. This can be a revelation for many athletes who are used to defining themselves as an athlete.

Let’s begin by defining the other “roles” in your life (other pursuits and areas of your life beyond sports). Your other roles can include student, businessperson, son or daughter, friend, wife or husband, father or mother, hobbyist, and so on. What are the other roles you have in your life? List at least five below.

Other Roles in my Life:

If your life is all about sport, you are at greater risk for self-esteem problems because you define yourself based on your level of success in sports and can’t separate the different roles in your life. You goal is to strive to find a balance in your life with family, school, sports, friends, hobbies, and other career aspirations.

  1. Become a Magician at "Role Playing" When you are playing tennis, involve yourself in that role - ccommit to 8 hours of practice or playing a day if thats what it takes, but when you leave the tennis court, switch roles and fully commit to other areas of your life. Dont superimpose the role of athlete onto other areas of life.
  2. Define Your Self-Concept Outside of SportsTo help you become a magician at changing roles, you must first define whou you are outside tennis(use appendix 1 to help you with this task.) Use only descriptions that apply to your personal characteristics that you bring to every aspect of your life. Make a list of these positive characteristics and review them every day. Know who the person inside is!
  3. Dont take it home with you.Leave tennis on the court. I know this one will be hard to do. At the very least, designate one or two hours of family or recreation time. Everyone needs a break. Leaving it on the court is especially important when you did not perform up to your ability. In addition, your family will appreciate you if you dont bring home the negative emotions.
  4. for Balance in your life. Success is what you define it to be. When I see athletes who have 24/7 involvement in their sport, I suggest that they find other hobbies, interests, or goals to occupy their time when not performing or practicing. Have you considered any hobbies that you were afraid to start because of the time demands of your sport? Do you have other career interests that you could spend a couple hours a week pursuing?
  5. List at least five other recreational activities, family gatherings, educational endeavors or career pursuits you enjoy doing on your free time. Make it a priority to involve yourself in these pursuits when not participating in sports:
  6. Others will not judge you on success in sports. Your immediate family and true friends love you for who you are as a person first. They don’t make judgments based on your win-loss record or change their view of you if you win or lose a game. Your true friends enjoy your company for what you bring to a relationship as a person, not as a successful athlete.
