Day 4 - Warm-up

The very first step is to identify the beliefs, attitudes, and expectations that cause you to hold onto your over-exactness in competition and lead to fear of failure. You want to keep the positive aspects of your mental game such as your motivation and commitment to sport. However, you most likely maintain beliefs or attitudes that support a fearful, cautious, or over-seriousness attitude when performing, such as “I must be perfect if I want to win today” or “I must analyze my mistakes and fix them right away so I don’t make the same mistake.” These may sound good, but actually hinder your ability to play well.

These behaviors (what I call core beliefs or attitudes) govern your non-functional perfectionistic thinking in competition and help to keep you stuck. Most of your expectations, especially perfectionistic ones do not help you perform freely in the present moment. Expectations and non-functional attitudes are often formed because of your past experience, coaching, and upbringing. Today, I want to help you uncover any beliefs that are causing you to stay stuck in a non-functional mindset.

Day 4 - Objective

To understand how beliefs, attitudes, and expectations about your performance support non-functional perfectionistic thinking in competition, and to help you identify these beliefs.

Day 4 - Fearless Strategy

Sometimes the truth hurts. However, having one or two perfectionistic beliefs in your sport does not label you as a perfectionist. The goal of today’s exercise is to help you uncover beliefs that sabotage your performance in competition and practice. You will need to take an honest look at your mental game to uncover the top ineffective beliefs I have listed in Table 5. This is not an exhaustive list, but are the most common ones athletes identify when I am working with them.

Table 5 Top non-functional believes in attitude

Ineffective belief
Effect on Performance
“I should demand high expectations of myself because I work so hard for my goals.”
I easily become frustrated and lose confidence when not reaching my expectations.
“It is impossible for me to work too much or too hard.”
This can cause me to over-train or burnout.
“I assess my performance as either good or bad with no middle ground” (all or none thinking).
I set myself up for failure before the competition begins and this can have a negative effect on my self-confidence.
“If my play is not perfect today, I must analyze it and fix it right away.”
Causes me to become over-analytical in competition and worry about technique.
“I must control my performance under pressure to make it happen the way I want.”
Causes me to over-control in competition and lack trust. Perform tight or scared.
“Making mistakes is unacceptable and I should be upset with myself.”
Causes me to try harder to perform perfectly, and become frustrated.
“The best way to perform perfectly is to avoid making mistakes.”
Leads me to think more about NOT making mistakes, and I can’t let go of mistakes.
“I want my performance to look good, feel correct, and get the results I desire.”
Causes me to be more perfect in everything and then lose confidence if it is not.
“My achievement in sports is more important than the person I am.”
Ego involvement. My self-worth is influenced by sports success or failure.
"Others will think I am an unworthy person unless I can prove I am successful in sports.”"
Causes me to judge my own self-worth based on what I assume others think.
"I should not try to do something unless I can do it perfectly."
Causes me to give up or not try altogether if performing poorly (e.g. tanking the match in tennis).
