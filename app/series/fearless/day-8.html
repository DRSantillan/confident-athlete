Day 8 - Warm-up

It is very common for perfectionistic athletes to worry too much about what others think of them. They seek social approval from others. They want to be admired, accepted, respected or liked by others. To some degree, this is normal because we are social beings who want to be liked by others.

Athletes who crave approval from others often become anxious or are afraid to fail in competition. They worry about public embarrassment. Perfectionists rely too much on what they think others think about them to boost their own feelings of self-worth. They associate self-worth with achievement in sports.

Perfectionistic athletes think they may lose approval from others by playing poorly or making mistakes. This worry supports an athlete’s fear of failure. Athletes are afraid to fail because they believe that there is potential for embarrassment, loss of respect, or not being liked by others!

Worrying too much about what others may think encourages a process I call “mind reading.” Mind reading is when you make assumptions about what others might be thinking about you. When you are trying to read others’ minds, you try to avoid embarrassment.

Day 8 - Objective

To learn how to stop worrying about what others think about your performance and remove the fear of embarrassment.

Day 8 - Fearless Strategy

The first step is to help you identify the specific type of social approval that causes you to mind read or worry too much about what others think. Please use Table 12 below to help you identify how you might worry about what others think.

Table 12. How Do You Worry About Others?

Worry
Description
Yes / No
1. You want to be liked by others
You use sports as a vehicle to make friends, but also want to be liked by others for performing well and being successful as a teammate.
2. You want to be respected
You think that others will respect you for being a successful achiever or performer.
3. You want to make others happy
You want to satisfy others. You may want to make a parent happy or play well for a coach.
4. You worry about embarrassment
You are afraid of embarrassment and feel anxious about embarrassing yourself.
5. You fear being rejected by others
You avoid taking risks or perform cautiously because you are afraid of rejection.
6. You have the need to feel popular
You crave notoriety from the public or peers – thinking that this enhances your image.
7. You want to impress others
You want to impress others with your sports skills – thinking that this will lead to positive feelings about yourself.

The goal of today’s lesson is to free you of the need for social approval, and thus free yourself from the fear. I want you to stop mind reading and making unfounded assumptions about what others might think. You most likely want the approval of other people so you can feel good about yourself as a person. You might use sports as an avenue to boost your feeling of self-worth and it sometimes backfires (when you don’t think you are getting it) due to failure or not being perfect in this case.

We start by defining your self-concept outside of sports. To help you with this, you should use the “Defining Your Self Concept” Worksheet I provide in Appendix 1. In this exercise, you want to:

  • Select the top 12 traits that describe your personal characteristics - beyond yourself as an athlete or without regard to your level of success. In other words, who is the person when not playing sports?
  • Ask two or three other people to complete this assignment individually based on what they know about you.
  • Use the results to help you develop a strong sense of self-concept that is based on your personal characteristics (not on your achievements).

Self-Esteem Should Not Be Based on Performance. Your self-esteem should not change based on what you assume others think about you or your success:

  • Use the results of “defining your self-concept” exercise completed in task 1 to help you.
  • The next step is to assess your self-concept. Do you like what you see with the results of this self-test? In other words, do you like who you are? This step is called an assessment of self-concept, and it’s related to self-esteem.
  • Your goal is to remember who you are no matter how you perform – refer back to the 12 personal characteristics you identified in step 1.
  • Finally, be clear that no matter how well or poorly you perform, people in your life do not change their opinions of who you are as a person! The people who matter most to you (e.g. true friends, family) will support you and their opinion of you is not based upon your achievements (or lack thereof) in sports.
  • You must have personal self-acceptance. Meaning you should accept yourself no matter how well or poorly you perform in sports. Then it will not matter what others think about how successful you are in sports.

Day 8 - Success Mottos

Repeat the following affirmations two times during the guided imagery exercise

  1. “I control my own destiny, not what I think others want for me.”
  2. “I am the only person who can give myself self-acceptance.”
  3. “I can recognize when I slip into avoidance mode and can adjust.”

Day 8 - Action Plans

  1. Recognize when you are mind reading or assuming what others think about you. Regroup and tell yourself that it does not matter what others think about you because you already think highly of yourself (self-acceptance).
  2. Remind yourself each day that you play for yourself and not to impress or gain the respect of other people including your parents and coaches.
