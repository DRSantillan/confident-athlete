Day 10 - Warm-up

Perfectionistic or super motivated athletes who love to train are the athletes who have trouble trusting their technique and performing fearlessly in competition. Perfectionists strive to look good on the playing fields. They want their performance to feel correct, technique to look good, and also get the correct results when they perform. I call this a "triple threat." If one of there areas are not perfect, you might become upset or over-analyze what went wrong.

Focusing too much on technique, looking good, or feeling correct prevents perfectionists from focusing on the real objective in sports - to win or get the job done efficiently as possible. When you are worried about how your game looks/feels , or desire to obtain perfect results, it is hard to play with freedom and a quiet mind.

I teach my students how to let go of the trying mindset and get into the flow mindset in competition by separating the practice athlete from the performer. This is the only way to find out if your technique is good enough.

Day 10 - Fearless Strategy

When I talk to my students about the practice and performance mindsets and learning to let if flow, I discuss the opposite of trust - over control. This helps you understand just how you sabotage your own trust. Table 14 below describes the top mental game breakdowns that sabotage your trust in competition.

Table 14. What Sabotages Your Trust?

Loss of Trust
Description
In training mindset
You can't stop training even in competition. Makes you think too much about the "how to" of your performance.
Overly precise mindset
You try to be too precise with your performance and it causes you to "guide" your body.
Overly technical mindset
You focus too much on technique, mechanics or making sure your performance feels perfect.
Trying too hard
You try too hard to go fast. Causes excessive tension in your performance and you lose flow or freedom of movement.
Afraid to make mistakes
You think about preventing mistakes and perform tentatively, which leads to trying too hard or over-control.
Trying to be perfect
You try too hard to be perfect with your performance, which leads to tension, over-control, and over-analysis.
