Day 13 - Warm-up

Up to this point in the workbook, I have discussed how to improve trust and perform without fear. In today's lesson, I want to help you understand some important practice strategies that will help you easily transition into the fearless performer mindset. As stated, perfectionists love to practice and work on weaknesses to the extent that practicing can undermine a performance mindset in competition. Some athletes are c-eat practice players, but can't apply their practice to competition.

One of the rules of trust is that you must know the best time to apply a training mindset and the best time to apply the performance mindset. This means you should also separate your practice into two phases. You might dedicate one part of your practice to improving your technique, mechanics, method, or working on drills. You might also dedicate a Portion of your practice to being a performer in the trust mindset - just as if you would perform in competition - similar to what team's do when they scrimmage or a basketball team has full court practice. Although it's hard to duplicate how yeu feel in the competitive environment, you should use part of your practice time to mimic (or recreate) what you will do in competition and strive for a performance mindset.

This helps you transition away from the training mindset into the performance mindset. For example, gymnasts do what is called "pressure sets" to help them match what happens in meets. Gymnasts use pressure sets to simulate the competitive environment when they p-actice so they can feel the same pressure of competition - or at least closer to what they will feel in competition. You do not have the use every second of practice to do drills to improve technique.

A misconception about practice is that you always have to be working on technique and mastering perfect mechanics. Your practices should include "performing" as well as drilling or training your technique. You need to put everything together into a whole performance. For example, basketball coaches will structure practice to work on drills for lay ups, free-throw technique, dribbling, passing, and defending. However, coaches also schedule scrimmages to allow athletes to apply the drill work to play. This is why football and basketball teams spend a lot of time scrimmaging and not just doing drills.
