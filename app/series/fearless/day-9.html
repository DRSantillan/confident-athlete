Day 9 - Warm-up

During mental toughness training, I teach my students about two mindsets that contribute to success in sports. The first is the training or practice mindset. Great athletes know the value of training. They strive to get better and to improve. They have a tremendous amount of motivation and work ethic, which help them to practice hard so they can master their skills.

The trusting or performance mindset is equally important for success in sports. Trust is your ability to let your skills “happen” instinctively by relying on your practice instead of consciously directing your movements. The performance mindset is the ability to rely on your practice, perform freely, and allow your skills to flow without excess thought.

The bottom line… If you are stuck in the practice mentality when you compete, you will limit your ability to perform your best because of too much analysis, trying to hard to be perfect, and a loss of trust.

Day 9 - Fearless Strategy

The first step is to understand how fear of failure and perfectionism sabotages your ability to perform freely (and fearlessly) in competition. Let’s start with a definition. The performance mindset (trust) is your ability to let go of consciously directing your body and allowing your well-learned physical skills to happen reflexively based on what you have practiced.

The concept of trust is different from self-confidence. Confidence is a belief in your ability that helps you play well in competition. Trust in your skills and your abilities. Fear of failure can undermine trust in many ways:

  • Perfectionists are mentally trapped in a practice mindset and thus have a hard time performing involuntarily in competition.
  • Perfectionists are afraid to fail and avoid making mistakes at all costs even resorting to playing too carefully.
  • Performing too carefully or defensively leads you to over-control your performance or technique; a lack of trust.
  • Since perfectionists think they need to perform their best, you might try too hard to be perfect with your game or performance.
  • The fear of making mistakes or disappointing others (fear of negative social scrutiny) also leads you to lose trust and not perform freely in competition.
  • Thus, fear of failure leads to trying to be perfect. Trying to be perfect causes over-control. Over-control leads to a lack of trust with your performance.
  • The cycle continues as you analyze what went wrong, work harder at fixing the errors, and then getting even more fixated in the training mindset.

Are you able to let it happen in competition? Are you stuck in the game of mastering your mechanics or technique and can’t play fearlessly in competition? Let’s find out!
