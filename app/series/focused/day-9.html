Day - Warm-up

How often do you go into a game unable to forget about an argument you had with a best friend or preoccupied with the exam you will be taking the next day? For some athletes, they bring life’s challenges and hassles with them when they enter the sports arena. One goal of this program is to help you enter the role of the focused athlete.

I teach my students to suspend their life outside of sports so they can focus completely on their performance – if only for 2 hours at a time. When you step onto the arena or practice facility, you must assume the role of athlete, the focused athlete. However, this does not mean you are ignoring real-life issues and challenges you are faced with at the time. Entering your role as athlete only means that you switch mental gears and now give yourself permission to focus on your sport without the mental handcuffs that you may otherwise bring into competition.

You can enter the role of the athlete by leaving life’s distractions in the parking lot and allowing yourself to be selfish for a couple hours. For example, to turn “off” life distractions and concentrate on your sport, write a task list of what you have to do after competition, (such as a school paper) then make a commitment to yourself to leave the list in the parking lot, your locker, or hotel room. After competition you can go back to your task list and do not need to worry about it during play.

Day - Daily Objective

To learn how to separate your challenges in life from your sports performance and how to enter the role of the focused athlete before competition.

Day - Daily Focus Boost

Although many athletes know how to suspend their daily hassles in life, I would like to discuss my plan for doing this. The first place to start is to understand what “distractions” from life you may bring into competition. Let us begin with some common examples:

  • Worrying about upcoming school projects which need to be completed or tests you need to pass.
  • Worrying about friendships because you spend so much time on your sport.
  • Thinking about the amount of work you have to do after your game or performance.
  • Having an unresolved issue in your life that causes emotional pain such as a divorce.
  • Being preoccupied with finance issues in your life.
  • Worrying about a sick child or another family member at home.
  • Dwelling about sponsors not signing you for a new season.
  • Failing to let go of an argument you had with your boss or co-worker.
Plan for Entering the Role of The Focused Athlete
1. The first task is to recognize what preoccupations (in your life) may hinder your focus in competition. What previous life events have distracted you from your game?
2. Agree to park any issues you are having in your life that may impact your focus. Here are some ideas to help you “park” personal issues:
  • “I can’t change my life right now, so I need to focus on my sport.”
  • “I can be selfish for 3 hours during the game. Think about what I need to do to help my team.”
  • “After the game, I will have more time to think about decisions I need to make in my life.”
  • “This is my time to have fun and let go of personal decisions.”
3. Commit yourself to focusing only on your sport and having fun. Remind yourself that only the present moment matters and to concentrate fully on execution.
4. Likewise you should also leave your sports performance on the field, court, or course so you can enjoy other areas of your life.

Day - Affirm Your Focus

You will be guided through a short period of relaxation (an abdominal breathing exercise) followed by a simple guided mental imagery exercise. In addition, you will repeat the following affirmations to yourself three times during the guided imagery exercise.

  1. “I will not let any matters in my life affect my sports performance.”
  2. “I am in complete control of what I choose to attend to.”
  3. “I am good at parking my personal problems during play.”

Day - Taking Focused Action

  • 20 minutes before you start practice or a game, notice if you are thinking about concerns from your life. Write these in your workbook.
  • Review or write down what you should focus on during the game to have a good performance. This can include strategy, game plan, process goals, and performance cues.
  • If you find yourself preoccupied with a problem outside of your sport, use your Three R’s to refocus.
  • You should also leave your performance on the field, court, or course when you go home or back to your hotel room.
