Day - Warm-up

One of the best methods I know to help athletes release their thoughts about outcomes and focus on the present moment is to give them simple process goals (also called mini-goals) to focus their attention to important cues during competition. My philosophy is this: Working the process brings desired results! This means you must put aside outcomes, results, and consequences of your performance and focus on the moment-to-moment execution. Ask yourself what you need to do moment-by-moment or play-by-play to perform your best.

One note here… I only use process goals to help you become immersed in the ingredients of the task. Although it’s rewarding to reach your goals, I don’t want you to evaluate or judge whether you’re on track to reach them. If you do this, you are not focusing on the process. The pre-game routine is an excellent time to decide on process goals to focus on during competition.

I use two types of process goals to help athletes stay in the moment:

  • Performance Goals: goals based on something you can measure such as, number of rebounds, blocked shots, or fairways hit in regulation.
  • Mental Goals: goals based on your mental game that help you focus on one play, shot, or routine at a time, such as committing to a plan or visualizing your pitch before you start the wind up.

Day - Daily Objective

To learn how to set process goals to help you stay focused on execution instead of outcomes.

Day - Daily Focus Boost

Each and every performance, your task is to set one or two process goals – both performance and mental process goals. In some sports such as triathlons, you can set process goals for each stage of the event (run, swim & bike). Let me start by giving you some sport specific examples of process goals or mini-goals that you can modify for your own sport.

Day - Affirm Your Focus

You will be guided through a short period of relaxation (an abdominal breathing exercise) followed by a simple guided mental imagery exercise. In addition, you will repeat the following affirmations to yourself three times during the guided imagery exercise.

  1. “Setting process goals helps me focus during the game.”
  2. “Results will take care of themselves when I focus on the process.”
  3. “I love the challenge of focusing on my process goals.”

Day - Taking Focused Action

  • Based upon your previous performance, think about two areas of your game you want to improve. Once you decide what you want to improve, set two process goals that will help you improve those two areas. Whatever you bring attention to during your performance, you will improve or at least think more about.
  • After each performance, review the game in your mind and assess your ability to stay focused on the process and your process goals.
