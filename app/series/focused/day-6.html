Day - Warm-up

As we discussed in the last lesson, the goal is to become fully immersed into your performance cues so that distracting thoughts do not have power to penetrate your focus. If you think of your attention as having a limited amount of space – in terms of what you can focus on at one time – with only enough capacity for what you want and need to focus on, it does not allow room for distractions to penetrate. However, this is the ideal state and not always the case when you are in a boring practice session or playing in a big game and under pressure. Thus, it is critical for you to learn how to refocus when you do have a distraction – and the first step is awareness.

Great athletes have enough awareness to know when they are in focus or slightly unfocused. Awareness is simply the ability to recognize when you are not focused, when the mind wanders, or when your mind is too active to concentrate. Therefore, the very first step in helping you get back on track after your mind strays is to catch yourself before you slip or make an error.

Day - Daily Focus Boost

Awareness can be both good and bad in sports. If you are too aware of your surroundings during a game to the point that your environment distracts you (i.e. a fan in the stands that is yelling), then you will not have total focus on your performance. However, for this daily concentration boost, I want you to have the awareness of the triggers that distract you, so you can stop what you are thinking about, and be able to make an adjustment.

The previous 2 sections should help you do this because you now know what you should and should not attend to when competing. This heightens your awareness of the wrong thoughts or cues to think about during practice or performance.

Most athletes have specific distractions they must deal with daily. I call these typical distractions. In addition, these distractions are triggered by some external “event” that happens to you when you are performing. A classic example is when you feel you got a bad call from an official. This can turn into an internal distraction, because you start to dwell on it and maybe even feel frustrated that you were on the end of a bad call, but this is something that is out of your control.

When you are aware of the triggers, you once again have a better chance to recognize that you need to make an adjustment mentally. Day - Affirm Your Focus

You will be guided through a short period of relaxation (an abdominal breathing exercise) followed by a simple guided mental imagery exercise. In addition, you will repeat the following affirmations to yourself three times during the guided imagery exercise.

  1. “I have the presence of mind to recognize when I am distracted.”
  2. “I am aware of the triggers that cause me to lose focus.”
  3. “I control my thoughts, behaviors and my focus.”

Day - Taking Focused Action

  • For each of these situations, write out how your thoughts changed from your performance cues to the distraction.
