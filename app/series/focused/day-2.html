Day - Warm-up

Every single athlete has the ability to concentrate, but sometimes what they focus on gets displaced or they think about the wrong stuff at the wrong time. Concentration can suffer from both internal and external distractions and can come in many forms from subtle to not so subtle. You must first identify your distractions during practice and competition.

What are internal and external distractions? The most common distractions are called “internal distractions.” These are self-induced disruptions in concentration that enter your mind in the form of doubt, indecision, lack of confidence, or thoughts about past events or future outcomes. Your own thinking, and not the environment or other people, triggers internal distractions.

The environment or what is going on outside your mind triggers external distractions. Some examples include people in the stands, harsh weather conditions, or an opponent that is trying to psych you out. External distractions start outside you, but can turn into internal distractions if not filtered correctly.

No matter what type of distraction, it will break your concentration and cause you to make errors. Understanding your potential concentration breakdowns – so you can recognize them sooner – is one of the important keys to superior concentration. You are not perfect and thus neither is your ability to stay focused 100% of the time. This means that you will need to adjust your concentration.

Day - Affirm Your Focus

You will be guided through a short period of relaxation (an abdominal breathing exercise) followed by a simple guided mental imagery exercise. In addition, you will repeat the following affirmations to yourself three times during the guided imagery exercise.

  1. “I am in control of my thoughts and can recognize when I become distracted.”
  2. “External distractions will only hurt my focus when I let them enter my mind.”
  3. “I choose to accept that distractions are a normal part of sports.”
