Day 1 - Warm-up

The pinnacle of a focused state of mind in sports is what we call being in “the zone.” What is the zone or a zone focus? Some athletes describe this state of awareness as flow, in a cocoon, or in a bubble. Regardless of how you describe the zone, it’s simply a state of total involvement in the task that you are performing in the present moment. We don’t need to make this complicated – it’s what you choose to attend to in your environment. And this changes from sport to sport and task to task within each sport.

My definition of focus is the ability to concentrate or pay attention to relevant tasks in the present moment without getting pulled off task by internal or external distractions (Later, I will define these two types of distractions). One note before I go any further, I use the words “focus” and “concentration” interchangeably.

Total concentration occurs when you become absorbed in the moment or task, lose track of time, and lose a sense of being separate from your sports experience. I know this sounds a little Zen like, but this ideal state is what I want you to understand, adopt and apply in your sport.

In addition, concentration is an ability to not attend to distractions or taskirrelevant cues that will upset your focus from the main objective of performing your best!

Thus, in summary, concentration or focus includes the following elements:

  1. Your ability to identify what’s relevant to focus on for peak performance in your sport
  2. Your ability to only focus on what is important to attend to for each task
  3. Your ability to stay focused in the present moment – or immersed
  4. Your ability to insulate yourself from distractions and an ability to refocus when you become distracted

Day 1 - Affirm Your Focus

You will be guided through a short period of relaxation (an abdominal breathing exercise) followed by a simple guided mental imagery exercise. In addition, you will repeat the following affirmations to yourself three times during the guided imagery exercise.

  1. “I am in control of my level of focus during performance.”
  2. “I choose to be focused during my performance even with distractions present.”
  3. “I control what I choose to focus on – not my competitors.”

Day 1 - Taking Focused Action

Why is understanding your zone state important? Everyone experiences a zone state differently. It is critical to understand how you feel and behave at those times when you have been totally focused. This is your template to use in the future to reproduce or reconstruct a state of total focus.

Based on the 5 or more qualities you listed in table 3, your task is to relive (mentally rehearse) what it felt like the last time you were in a state of total focus. Use the questions below to help you recall the important thoughts, feelings, and images when you were in a heightened state of focus during a mini mental rehearsal.

Questions to Consider:

  1. Recall the last time you were totally focused.
  2. What happened before the game or performance?
  3. What were you thinking about before it started?
  4. How would you describe your focus?
  5. What did it feel like when you re-lived this experience?
  6. What was different about this time from your typical experience when performing?
  7. Did you feel tired or energized during this performance?
  8. What emotions did you feel during this performance?
  9. Did time seem to slow down or go by faster than normal?
  10. How did a state of focus affect your performance?

Can you see yourself performing with this same level of focus in your next game or performance? The goal is to be able to harness this level of focus every time you step onto the court, field, or track. One way to do this is to recall what it felt like in the past and rehearse this feeling for the next game or performance.
