Day - Warm-up

A pre-shot routine is an excellent way to integrate your focusing skills into your regular practice and competition. For the purpose of this workbook, there are two goals of the pre-shot routine. The first is to improve the consistency of your mental preparation in the hopes of leading to a more consistent performance. The second purpose is to help you focus on performance cues and thus your execution. Preshot routines are best applied to self-paced tasks (stop-and-go sports, such as bowling) instead of continuous-play sports (such as swimming). However, in continuous sports, athletes can also apply routines to prepare for the start of a performance, also called pre-performance routines, such as a swimmer on the blocks before the start of a race or a hockey player starting a new shift.

What is most important is having a good mental approach or an effective mental routine. The mental routine directly ties into your performance cues that you must focus on for successful execution. In fact, your performance cues actually comprise the elements of your routine.

A mental pre-shot routine works to help you to:

  1. Observe and process information.
  2. Make decisions, set strategies, and develop a plan.
  3. Program your mind and body to perform well.
  4. Mentally rehearse the skill you are about to execute.
  5. Get your mind in an optimal state to execute your best.

If you neglect one of these areas in your preparation, you fail to prepare fully for successful execution. The objective is to train yourself to approach each shot or start of your performance with good planning, confidence, and focus on performance cues.

Day - Daily Objective

To learn how to use a pre-shot routine to help you focus on the proper performance cues for execution in a self-paced task.

Day - Daily Focus Boost

Your task is to develop a pre-shot or pre-performance routine that defines the appropriate performance cues for execution. There are 6 steps in the mental routine you want to follow in order to prepare for a shot, kick, swim, or field goal for example. Below is a golf specific example:

  1. Planning: Select a visual target and the type of shot you want to hit. Be specific and commit to the plan.
  2. Image: See or feel the type of shot you selected in the plan. Make it seem as real as possible by feeling the shot or by visualizing the shot trajectory. (e.g., hit the green and roll into the hole).
  3. Rehearse: Use a practice swing to feel the shot you want to hit. Focus on balance or the simple feeling of the swing.
  4. Focus: Stay focused on execution and the visual or feel of the shot.
  5. Set up: Aim the clubface and set your body parallel to the left of the clubface.
  6. Trust: Focus on the target and let the image of the target or the feeling of a good swing trigger the swing. No more than one swing should be thought about here.

Depending on your sport or the task you are performing, these steps will change. For example, for a tennis serve, the steps in the set up will differ from the golf example above. In addition, the images you use to see or feel successful execution will be specific to your learning preferences.

Day - Affirm Your Focus

You will be guided through a short period of relaxation (an abdominal breathing exercise) followed by a simple guided mental imagery exercise. In addition, you will repeat the following affirmations to yourself three times during the guided imagery exercise.

  1. “I have a focused routine that I can trust in competition.”
  2. “My routine helps me focus only on execution and block out all distractions.”
  3. “Nothing can penetrate my focus when I am focused on my routine.”

Day - Taking Focused Action

  • Athletes use a variety of images (visual, feeling, auditory, etc.) to help them develop the proper mindset for optimal performance. To make the most of imagery, you will need to tap into the correct pre-shot images (based on your style of learning and performing). In your next practice, test the different images you can use in your preshot routine to help you find the ones that work best for you.
  • Be aware of how your routine breaks down during your performance. Do you get lazy, distracted, indecisive, or anxious during the routine?
