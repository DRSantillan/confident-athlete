Day - Warm-up

The next step is to define the thoughts, feelings, and environmental cues that are not important (also called task-irrelevant cues that have no relevance to your performance). You need to be able to distinguish between what is important to focus on for execution and what is just a distraction to your performance. The list of non-relevant information is much longer than what is relevant to your performance.

Irrelevant information in the form of internal or external distractions can cloud your focus and prevent you from focusing on the task at hand. If irrelevant cues or distractions go unchecked in your mind, your concentration and performance will suffer. Before you realize it, a small distraction can cause you to miss an important free throw or miss an easy putt, for example, if you are trying to hit a putt and you have a thought related to what you need to do after the round pops into your head.

What are task-irrelevant cues or thoughts? As an athlete, your mind is bombarded with a multitude of thoughts, feelings, emotions, and external information in which you have to filter and attend to the important stuff. For example, thinking about what you will do after the hockey game has no bearing on the current play you are running in the game. Thus, it’s not important, nor relevant to helping you execute a play.

Day - Affirm Your Focus

You will be guided through a short period of relaxation (an abdominal breathing exercise) followed by a simple guided mental imagery exercise. In addition, you will repeat the following affirmations to yourself three times during the guided imagery exercise.

  1. “I choose to park any distractions before I compete.”
  2. “I am prepared, focused, and ready to compete in today’s event.”
  3. “I can quickly identify distractions so I can change my focus.”

Day - Taking Focused Action

  • The next time you have practice or competition, notice any information that runs through your awareness that has no relevance to your performance.
  • Record your irrelevant or distracting thoughts after the practice or competition in your workbook or a journal.
