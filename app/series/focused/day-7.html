Day - Warm-up

My formula for superior focus - and regaining your focus when it’s lost - comes together in today’s lesson as we begin to put all the previous lessons to work with a strategy called the “3 R’s for Refocusing.” The Three R’s are: Recognize, Regroup and Refocus.

A Simple 3 Step Method for Refocusing After Distractions:

  • Recognize — awareness that you are not on task or distracted.
  • Regroup — ability to break the current distraction or thought.
  • Refocus — to get back on the performance cues for the next play or shot.

The three R’s are designed to help you regain your focus as quickly as possible after a distraction. The moment you notice that you are off-task, it is highly important to regain concentration by having the ability to pull your focus back to your performance cues or to get back in the present.

This entire process should not be lengthy and take no more than 2-4 seconds to complete quickly “on the fly.” With some self-paced tasks such as a golf shot, tennis serve, or free throw in basketball, I recommend to students that they stop and restart their routine over if they get distracted at the wrong time.

This may not be possible in some sports with continuous action, like hockey or basketball in which you are reacting to an opponent. That’s why you should quickly try and refocus on the fly. You have to learn to quickly recognize distractions and equally as fast, get your head back into the task.

Day - Daily Focus Boost

The first R in the Three R’s deals with awareness, which I discussed in lesson 6. The second R (Regroup) is the transition between awareness (“I am not focused”) to action (“I need to get my head in the game”). The third R is the most important step, because this is how you will immerse yourself into your performance after a short brain fade.

I am not talking about brain surgery here – Recognize – Regroup – and Refocus. What are some ways you can regroup so you can begin to refocus?

  • “STOP! – that thought is not relevant.”
  • “GET BACK – this is not important.”
  • “Get your head in the game!”
  • “Stop dwelling in the past!”
  • “Forget about the future!”
  • “I control my own thoughts and focus.”

I think you get the idea above. You want to use a short statement to zap you into the present moment as a way of getting you ready to re-engage with your performance cues. Here is a good question you can ask yourself to help you with the final and most important R (Refocus): “What do I need to focus on right now to execute this play, shot, or routine?” You might also use a physical cue to mentally trigger yourself to refocus. Many athletes use physical triggers to start an at-bat routine for example, or to get refocused as well, such as tapping the bat on the plate at the start of an at-bat. Another example is when a golfer tightens her golf glove, which is symbolic to tightening her focus for the shot.

Day - Affirm Your Focus

You will be guided through a short period of relaxation (an abdominal breathing exercise) followed by a simple guided mental imagery exercise. In addition, you will repeat the following affirmations to yourself three times during the guided imagery exercise.

  1. “I am in control of my focus and can regain my concentration at will.”
  2. “I will commit to using the Three R’s when I get distracted.”
  3. “I can handle any distraction because I can refocus.”

Day - Taking Focused Action

  • The next time you practice or compete, use your Three R strategies to help you refocus and get back on task.
  • Continue to refine these strategies until you find the best approach to helping you quickly and effortlessly get your head back into the game.
